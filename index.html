Introduce the Big Question

How do I show people who I am?

Write the Big Question on chart paper and have students brainstorm how they show who they are, such as hair, clothes, language, art, or other forms of expression. Use a word web like the one below to capture students’ thoughts and ideas. Display the word web throughout the lesson.

Mirrors & Windows

My Avatar Draw and share a picture of yourself as an avatar. Point out features of your avatar that show who you are. For example, "My avatar is wearing a suit because I feel confident when I wear a suit and tie."

Then, have students draw themselves as avatars and discuss with a partner. Be sensitive to students who may be reluctant to focus on aspects of their appearances. See Activity Support for more information.

First Read Aloud: Hair Love

  • 1. Before reading, hold up Hair Love and introduce the author, illustrator, and point out some of the sound-word vocabulary. Tell students that this book was made into a movie that won an Academy Award.

Second Read Aloud

  • 1. Before reading, hold up Hair Love and remind students that they have already read this book. Ask student volunteers to tell you some things they know about Zuri.
  • 2. During reading, periodically stop and ask students questions about the problem, or conflict, and more pointed questions that challenge students to infer what Daddy and Mommy might be thinking or feeling, such as:
    • Why is Daddy so tired?
    • Who is Daddy talking about when he says, “We learned from the best”?
    • What clues tell you that Mommy is not at home?
    • How does Mommy feel when she sees Zuri?

Author’s Craft

Word Sort: Sound Words

Remind students of the sound words (onomatopoeia) they learned before reading Hair Love and point out examples from the text.

  • 1. shh (Daddy is sleeping)
  • 2. crack (Zuri drops the tablet)
  • 3. click (the door opens)
  • 4. flash (Zuri is taking a picture with tablet camera)

Write the word Onomatopoeia on the board and discuss why authors use onomatopoeia in their writing. Explain that authors use onomatopoeia for many reasons, including to describe sounds, create interest, and play with language. Tell students that they will work together to complete a word sort using the sound word cards they created before reading Hair Love. Directions for the word-sort activity are included in Activity Support.

Third Read Aloud

  • 1. Before reading, hold up the book and ask students to tell the title. Then, ask for volunteers to tell what the problem is and how it is solved.
  • 2. During reading, focus on the following pages to highlight some of the key events from the beginning, middle, and end of the story.

    • Middle: Starting with "Daddy gathered all the tools we needed, and we were set!"

  • What is Zuri feeling?

Reflect on the Big Question

How do I show people who I am?

Display the word web from the before reading activity. Remind students of the ideas they brainstormed before reading Hair Love. Ask students to think about and discuss the different ways the characters in Hair Love show who they are. Add any new ideas to the word web.

Mirrors & Windows

Find the following Hair Love quotes in the book; read each quote aloud while displaying the corresponding illustration. After each quote, have students turn to a partner and tell whether the quote is a mirror (an experience that reflects their own life) or a window (a view into another person’s experience). Use the third quote to model a Think Aloud. Have students complete the sentence frame for each quote.

  • "I have hair that has a mind of its own."
  • "Because today is special, I want a perfect hairstyle."
  • "My hair is Mommy, Daddy, and me."

This quote is a mirror/window for me because blank line ────────── .

Sample Think Aloud: This is a window for me because I learned that for many people hair and hairstyles are a way to express pride in their family and culture.

Share & Celebrate

Prepare to Share

Provide time for students to practice sharing their culminating project, reminding them that each partner should have a significant role in sharing the information. Partners should work collaboratively and follow agreed upon rules, norms, and protocols.

Use the bulleted points below to model good speaking and listening skills.

  • When I share my project, I look at the audience. I don’t keep my eyes down.
  • As I speak, I make sure that everyone can hear what I am saying.
  • I pronounce each word clearly and correctly.
  • I do not rush, but speak clearly and at a natural pace.
  • I pay attention with my eyes and ears when others are speaking.

Celebrate!

Have students share their projects with the class. As students share, be sure to model and encourage strong listening skills.

 

Title: Hair Love

Author: Matthew A. Cherry

Illustrator: Vashti Harrison

Genre: Realistic Fiction

Lexile: AD480

Page Count: 32

Publisher: Penguin Random House

Copyright: 2019

Plot Overview

Zuri is a 5-year-old African American girl with hair that has a mind of its own. At the beginning of the story we learn that this is a big day for Zuri. Her mother is coming home and she needs the perfect hairstyle.

However, Zuri’s dad is exhausted from working, so Zuri decides to fix her own hair. When Dad wakes up, he offers to help fix Zuri’s hair for her special day. Together, through trial and error, the two experiment with different hairstyles.

By the end of the story, Zuri and her dad come up with the perfect hairstyle (with the help of an online video). With her new hairstyle Zuri feels like a superhero. In the last scene we see her rushing to the door to welcome home her mother.

Theme Overview

Hair Culture Our hair and hairstyles are part of who we are and how we express ourselves. Beautiful hair might be any color, thin or thick, straight or curly, short or long, braided or twisted or loose. For many African Americans, like Zuri, the main character in Hair Love, her natural hair is a symbol of pride and self esteem. As Zuri says, “My hair is Mommy, Daddy, and me. It’s hair love!”

Family Relationships The book also explores family relationships, and how people express their love for one another. One way that Zuri’s father shows his love for her is by learning how to help Zuri style her natural hair. This book celebrates how their bond grows stronger as they spend quality time together, united in the common goal of helping Zuri achieve “perfect hair” in time for her mother’s return, thus showing their love for one another and for Mommy.

Activity Support

Sound Words

  • 1. Distribute notecards with one sound word from the text written on each card—give each student one card.
  • 2. Tell students to draw a picture representing the sound word on their card.
  • 3. Have students share and compare their drawings.
  • 4. Write the word Onomatopoeia on the board. Have students echo the word as you pronounce the word. Explain that onomatopoeia refers to words that describe sounds.
  • 5. Then, distribute blank notecards and have students work with a partner to name and draw other sound words they know. Encourage students who speak another language to name and draw sound words from their home languages.
  • 6. Collect the cards. Create a class set of onomatopoeia flashcards, display them in the classroom, or incorporate them into a class book.

Activity Support

Word Sort: Sound Words

  • 1. Choose six to ten sound word cards from the before reading activity.
  • 2. Hold up each card and invite the students to act out the sound together.
  • 3. After reviewing each word, tape the sound word cards on the board or place them in a pocket chart.
  • 4. Then, lead students in brainstorming three to four categories for grouping the sound words (e.g. loud sounds, soft sounds, animal sounds).
  • 5. Write the categories (on the board or on cards) and ask students which sound words belong to each category.
  • 6. Move the cards under the appropriate category until all the sound words have been sorted.

*Some sound words may belong to more than one category. Consider all ideas and encourage students to explain their thinking.

Activity Support

Unlock a Key Sentence

Display and read aloud the key sentence. Then guide students in unlocking its meaning by discussing each sentence chunk using the protocol described below.

Start with the sentence chunk that tells who or what the sentence is mostly about. Continue to unpack meaning and connect ideas, chunk by chunk, using the guiding questions in the table below.

Because today is special | I want | the perfect hairstyle.

Sentence Chunk Discuss Meaning
Because today is special

-What is the sentence about? (today)

-What does the speaker tell us about today? (it is special)

-Why is it special? (we don't know yet)

-What does the word "because" signal? (it signals cause and effect; the speaker expects or wants something because it is a special day)
I want

-Who is the speaker? (Zuri)

-What other words could Zuri use besides "want"?(need, wish for, hope for)
the perfect hairstyle.

-Why does the author italicize the word "perfect"? (to emphasize or call attention to the importance of looking her best)

-Why does Zuri want the perfect hairstyle?(because today is special)

-Do we know why today is special? (we still don’t know — the author is building anticipation)

After unlocking the key sentence, have students craft their own sentence using the following sentence frame:

Because today is ────────── I want the perfect ────────── .

 

Activity Support

Setting Switch

Ask the following questions to support and guide discussion:

  • What are some details from the story that are specific to the setting of Bangladesh? (the rickshaw, Mother’s gold bangle, etc.)
  • If Rickshaw Girl took place in a different country, city, or town, how might these details change? (instead ofa rickshaw, the father might drive a taxi)
  • How might this change the plot?

Activity Support

Author’s Craft: Word Sort

In this activity, students work together to complete a word sort and discuss the author’s use of adverbs to convey emotion and describe vivid details.

Follow the steps:

  • 1. Display the following list of adverbs from the text: fiercely, quickly, quietly, gradually, soundlessly, desperately, silently, slowly, wildly
  • 2. Using the chart below, have pairs or groups of students work together to sort the list of adverbs into three categories.

     

    Describes Sound Describes Speed Describes Intensity

    *Answer key is included below.

  • 3. Review student responses and discuss how adverbs might be used to describe vivid details and/or convey specific emotions (e.g. desperately conveys an intense emotion).
  • 4. Display the following sentences from the text and tell students to rewrite each sentence using a different adverb. Students may use adverbs from the word sort or other adverbs they know.
    • The wheels in Naima’s mind were spinning wildly. (p. 25)
    • Desperately Naima tried to turn the rickshaw, but she couldn’t seem to change direction. (p. 31)
    • Slowly, Mother eased a bangle over her hand and handed it to Father. (p. 46)

  • 5. Have students compare their sentences with a partner and discuss how different adverbs affect the meaning and emotion conveyed in each of the sentences.

    Answer Key:

    Describes Sound Describes Speed Describes Intensity
    quietly quickly fiercely
    silently gradually wildly
    soundlessly slowly desperately

Activity Support

Author’s Craft: Loan Words

Draw students’ attention to the use of Bangla words in Rickshaw Girl and discuss how these words enhance our understanding of the setting.

Explain that there are many words from other languages, called loan words, that are now part of the English language.

Brainstorm some examples with students (e.g. karaoke from Japanese, ballet from French, siesta from Spanish, etc.) and then have students search the internet for other examples. Have students share two to three interesting examples with the class.
